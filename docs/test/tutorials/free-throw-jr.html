5. Position the Player Character at the bottom of the screen

Let‚Äôs move the athlete down to give it room to grow.

  • :paper plane: From the ||sprites: Sprites|| category in the toolbox, grab
    ||sprites: set [athlete] position to x [80] y [90]||
    and snap it inside at the end of the ||loops(noclick): on start|| block already in your workspace.

~hint Why use 80 and 90? üí°

The width of the screen is 160 pixels, so an x value of 80 will place the athlete in the middle of the screen from left to right.

The y value of 90 will put the athlete near the bottom of the screen because the screen height is 120 pixels.

These numbers were carefully selected to put the athlete in the right place for the next several steps‚Ä¶but you can also play around with the values to see what happens when you make them larger or smaller.

hint~

scene.setBackgroundImage(throw_imgs.court)
let athlete = sprites.create(throw_imgs.dunk, SpriteKind.Player)
//@highlight
athlete.setPosition(80, 90)

6. Set the player‚Äôs velocity (movement)

In this game, your player will be moving back and forth.

  • :paper plane: From ||sprites:Sprites||, drag
    ||sprites:set [athelete] velocity to vx [50] vy [0]||
    to the end of the
    ||loops(onclick):on start|| container.

~hint What are vx and vy?üí°

In MakeCode Arcade, vx stands for the ‚Äúvelocity in the direction of x‚Äù ‚Äî which is a complicated way of saying ‚Äúthe speed from side to side.‚Äù

  • The larger your vx value is, the faster something will travel to the right.
  • The lower your vx, the faster it will travel to the left.
  • If your vx is 0, the item won‚Äôt move from side to side at all.

Similarly, vy stands for the ‚Äúvelocity in the direction of y‚Äù ‚Äî which is a complicated way of saying ‚Äúthe speed from top to bottom.‚Äù

  • The larger your vy value is, the faster something will travel downward.
  • The lower your vy, the faster it will travel upward.
  • If your vy is 0, the item won‚Äôt move either up or down.

hint~

scene.setBackgroundImage(throw_imgs.court)
let athlete = sprites.create(throw_imgs.dunk, SpriteKind.Player)
athlete.setPosition(80, 90)
//@highlight
athlete.setVelocity(50, 0)

11. Shoot the Basketballs

Add the basketball projectiles.

~hint What are projectiles?üí°

In MakeCode Arcade, projectiles are sprites that move on their own, often in large quantities.

Projectiles have extra properties that normal sprites don‚Äôt have. For example, they destroy themselves once they leave the screen so the user‚Äôs computer doesn‚Äôt get overwhelmed.

hint~

  • :paper plane: Snap
    ||variables(sprites):set [projectile] to projectile [üèÄ] from [athlete] with vx [0] vy [-100]||
    inside the empty
    ||controller(noclick):on [A] button pressed ||
    container.

üí° The projectile is a basketball, but you can click the ball and change it to whatever you like.

let hoop = sprites.create(throw_imgs.hoop, SpriteKind.Goal)
hoop.setPosition(80, 35)
controller.A.onEvent(ControllerButtonEvent.Pressed, function () {
    projectile = sprites.createProjectileFromSprite(throw_imgs.ball, athlete, 0, -100)
})

~hint Why ‚Äúdestroy sprite‚Äù? üí°

You may think we should destroy projectile or even ball to get rid of the basketball that overlapped the hoop, but this overlap container requires you to choose either
||variables:sprite|| or ||variables:otherSprite||
when you want to select one of the exact items that touched.

hint~

let hoop = sprites.create(throw_imgs.hoop, SpriteKind.Goal)
hoop.setPosition(80, 35)
sprites.onOverlap(SpriteKind.Projectile, SpriteKind.Goal, function (sprite, otherSprite) {
    info.changeScoreBy(1)
    sprites.destroy(sprite)
})
